### 1. Preserve-list

### 2. Steelman of Current Choices
*   **Choice: Combining "Performance" and "Fixes" into one section.**
    *   *Steelman:* Keeping minor bug fixes and performance improvements in a single, final section prevents the document from feeling cluttered or overly negative. It ensures the reader's primary focus remains on the high-value feature additions (Search, Reviews, Admin).
*   **Choice: Stating "180 days retention on all tiers" without tier differentiation.**
    *   *Steelman:* By offering 180 days of activity logs even on free/lower tiers, Meridian positions itself as highly generous and transparent. It avoids complex tier-comparison matrices in a simple release announcement.
*   **Choice: Omitting visual aids (screenshots/GIFs) or links to documentation.**
    *   *Steelman:* This keeps the release notes lightweight, fast-loading, and highly portable across different distribution channels (e.g., email newsletters, in-app changelogs, markdown files).

### 3. Author's Goal & Stress Test
*   **The Goal:** To inform existing customers (users, admins, and developers) of the new capabilities in Meridian 2.4, reassure them of a seamless upgrade path, and drive immediate adoption of the new search and review features without generating support tickets.
*   **Stress Test against alternative reading:** 
    *   *Alternative reading:* The goal is to serve as a marketing/sales landing page to attract new enterprise leads.

Instead, it focuses on granular technical details like "sidebar hydration off the critical path," exact query operators (`from:name`), and REST API endpoint paths (`/activity`).

### 4. Assumptions
*   **Assumption 1:** Meridian is a collaborative document/knowledge management platform (similar to Notion or Confluence).
    *   *If wrong:* If Meridian is an IDE, a database tool, or an ERP, terms like "document body text," "review workflows," and "editor" are misapplied, and the entire framing of the release notes is incorrect.
*   **Assumption 2:** The new REST API endpoints (`/activity` and `/reviews`) use the same authentication and permission models as existing 2.x endpoints.
    *   *If wrong:* Developers attempting to use these endpoints will experience immediate authorization failures, leading to an influx of developer support tickets.
*   **Assumption 3:** Self-hosted customers have a standard upgrade process that matches the SaaS database schema.
    *   *If wrong:* Self-hosted databases might fail during the in-place upgrade, causing downtime and critical data loss.
